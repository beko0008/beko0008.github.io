Life of an Eagle

Eagles are among the most powerful and awe-inspiring birds on the planet, revered across cultures and civilisations for thousands of years as symbols of strength, freedom, and vision. Belonging to the family Accipitridae, eagles are large birds of prey found on every continent except Antarctica, inhabiting a wide range of environments from dense tropical forests and open grasslands to high mountain ranges and coastal cliffs. There are more than 68 recognised species of eagle, the most well-known of which include the bald eagle, the golden eagle, the harpy eagle, and the Philippine eagle. Eagles are defined by their powerful hooked beaks, strong muscular legs, and sharp curved talons which they use to seize and kill prey with incredible force. Their eyesight is among the sharpest in the animal kingdom — an eagle can spot a rabbit from a distance of over three kilometres, and their eyes are so large relative to their skull that they cannot move them in their sockets, instead turning their entire head to track movement. Eagles are built for soaring; their broad wings generate lift with minimal effort, allowing them to ride thermal currents of warm air high into the sky and cover vast distances while barely flapping. The bald eagle, the national symbol of the United States, was once on the brink of extinction due to hunting and the widespread use of the pesticide DDT, which caused their eggshells to thin and break before hatching. Thanks to conservation efforts and legal protections, bald eagle populations have made a remarkable recovery and the species was removed from the endangered list in 2007. The harpy eagle, found in the rainforests of Central and South America, is considered the most powerful eagle in the world, capable of snatching monkeys and sloths directly from the treetops with its massive talons which can exert a grip force comparable to a large dog bite. Eagles typically mate for life and return to the same nest year after year, adding new material each season until the nest becomes enormous — the largest bald eagle nest on record weighed nearly three tonnes and measured almost three metres across.

Golden eagles are among the most widely distributed eagle species in the world, found across North America, Europe, Asia, and parts of Africa. They are supremely agile hunters capable of diving at speeds exceeding 240 kilometres per hour when pursuing prey, which includes rabbits, hares, ground squirrels, and occasionally young deer or mountain goats. In many parts of Central Asia, golden eagles have been trained by nomadic peoples for centuries to hunt foxes, hares, and even wolves, a tradition known as falconry that continues to this day and is recognised by UNESCO as an intangible cultural heritage.

Facts About Eagles

  • Eagles have hollow bones which reduce their weight and make sustained flight much easier despite their large size.
  • The Philippine eagle is the largest eagle in the world by length and wingspan and is critically endangered with fewer than 800 individuals remaining.
  • An eagle's talon grip is estimated to be ten times stronger than the grip of an average human hand.
  • Eagles have two foveae in each eye, giving them both forward and side vision simultaneously with extraordinary clarity.
  • Some eagle species can live for up to 30 years in the wild and even longer in captivity.
